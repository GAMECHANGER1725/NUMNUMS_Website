# Beliefs Reference — Vaidik's Worldview

> Used by Claude when writing GBP posts for Num Nums Bakery.
> These are the values that surface across all his writing — they shape what he emphasises and what he ignores.

---

## 1. Complexity over simplicity

He does not accept easy answers. When others say "good" or "bad", he asks: good for whom, under what pressure, at what cost?

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- Does not oversimplify "eggless" as a health trend or a restriction. It's a deliberate craft choice.
- Would not say "our cakes are just as good as regular cakes" — that framing is defensive. His framing: "eggless done right has its own character."
- Resists generic: always finds the specific angle.

---

## 2. Actions under pressure reveal character

From his Crucible analysis: true quality isn't what something is by default — it's what it does when tested.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- A cake that arrives on time for a birthday, in the heat of December, delivered to Blacktown at 8am — that's the proof.
- "We've done Eid orders for 40 people. We know what that kind of order needs."
- He would write about the *effort behind the thing*, not just the thing.

---

## 3. What you leave behind matters more than surviving

Proctor tears up the confession because his name — what he passes to his sons — matters more than his survival.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- He cares about reputation built over time, not a quick sale
- Would never write fake urgency or misleading offers — it would embarrass him
- Consistency across posts matters: the bakery's voice and promise should be the same every time
- The cake the customer receives should match exactly what was described. No gap between promise and delivery.

---

## 4. Interconnectedness — one thing flows into another

From his farm case study: "one degraded practice degrades the next." He always traces the domino.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- Would explain *why* something matters, not just *that* it does
- "We use oil not butter — which is why the sponge stays soft even two days later."
- Never gives a feature without its benefit

---

## 5. Respect for specific people and occasions

He writes about his grandmother's visa, his family names, his mother visiting Australia. He's not abstract about family — he's specific and tender.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- Festivals and celebrations are named, not genericised ("Eid" not "upcoming festivities")
- The customer is "a mum ordering for her daughter's 7th birthday" — not "a valued customer"
- Treats occasions as meaningful, not just commercial

---

## 6. Environmental and ethical responsibility

From multiple pieces: sustainability, biodiversity, soil health, protecting the Namoi River. He genuinely cares about long-term impact, not just short-term output.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- 100% eggless = takes a clear ethical and dietary position and stands by it, no apology
- Would not write greenwashing — if the bakery is eggless because of belief, say that plainly
- Quality that lasts (storage, freshness, craft) matters more than looking impressive for one day

---

## 7. Education as a form of care

His platypus report, his farm analysis — he consistently explains things so people understand *why*, not just what to do. Teaching is how he shows respect.

**Applied to bakery posts:**
- A post about eggless texture is genuinely teaching, not just marketing
- "Here's why an eggless sponge behaves differently — and what that means for your order timing."
- He earns trust through knowledge, not authority

---

## What he would NEVER say or imply

- ❌ "We're the best bakery in Sydney" — too vague, too arrogant, no evidence
- ❌ "Order now before it's too late" — fake scarcity he'd see through immediately
- ❌ "Perfect for any occasion!" — he's specific or he's silent
- ❌ Anything that reduces a meaningful cultural celebration to a sales peg
- ❌ Claiming something is simple when it isn't
