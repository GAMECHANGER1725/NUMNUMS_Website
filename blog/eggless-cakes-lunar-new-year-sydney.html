Why Does Eggless Matter for Lunar New Year Gatherings?

Egg is one of Australia's priority food allergens under Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) labelling rules, and it's a particularly common allergen in young children — the very guests most likely to be passed the first slice at a family reunion. An entirely egg-free kitchen removes the cross-contamination worry that comes with a bakery using eggs in its other products.

From our experience through past Lunar New Year periods, the orders that go smoothest are the ones placed early with clear notes — a colour scheme, a flavour, and any allergies in the room. We've decorated cakes for reunion dinners where a grandparent keeps a Buddhist vegetarian diet and a grandchild has an egg allergy at the same table. One eggless cake covered both, with no awkward substitutions.

How Does an Eggless Cake Fit Alongside Traditional Lunar New Year Sweets?

From our internal order data across past Lunar New Year periods, a Western-style celebration cake almost always sits beside the traditional sweets, not in place of them — most festival orders pair our cake with a reunion table that still features nian gao and other classics. The cake has simply become the shared dessert and the photo centrepiece of the night.

Nian gao (年糕), the sticky rice cake whose name sounds like "higher year", symbolises progress and rising fortune; tang yuan and other sweets carry their own meanings of family togetherness. A decorated eggless sponge doesn't compete with any of that. It adds a modern, sliceable centrepiece that photographs beautifully and, crucially, that every guest at the table can actually eat.

Isn't that the real appeal of an eggless cake here? A reunion already balances old and new across three generations. A red-and-gold eggless cake bridges both worlds — traditional colours and symbolism on the outside, a soft modern crumb that suits vegetarian elders and egg-allergic grandchildren alike on the inside. It's exactly why the lighter, fruit-forward flavours like Mango and Lychee tend to lead: they feel celebratory next to richer traditional sweets without overwhelming the table.

Are These Cakes Suitable for Dietary and Cultural Needs?

Every cake is 100% eggless and made in a fully egg-free kitchen, which suits several dietary frameworks common across Sydney's Lunar New Year communities — lacto-vegetarian, Jain, and egg-allergic eaters among them. That said, honesty matters: our cakes are egg-free, not allergen-free.

  • Vegetarian and Buddhist households — many exclude eggs; our entire menu is egg-free by default.
  • Egg allergy — our egg-free kitchen removes the egg cross-contamination pathway; for severe or anaphylactic allergy, tell us when ordering and follow your own medical and ASCIA-aligned advice.
  • Contains dairy — our cakes use milk powder and butter, so they're not vegan.
  • May involve nuts — flavours like Ferrero Rocher contain nuts; always flag nut allergies when you order.
  • Contains wheat — standard cakes are not gluten-free.

If you have specific allergen questions beyond eggs, message us before ordering and we'll give a full ingredient breakdown for your chosen flavour.
