What Are Number Cakes — and Why Are They So Popular in Sydney?

A number cake is exactly what it sounds like: a cake built in the shape of a number, so a 40th birthday gets a literal "40" on the table. Most are made from layered sponge cut to the digit's outline, stacked with cream filling, and finished with piped cream rosettes, fresh berries, chocolate pieces, macarons or edible flowers. The shape does the storytelling — no message plaque required.

Why have they taken over Sydney parties? Two reasons. First, they're unmistakably personal: the cake announces the milestone at a glance, which is gold for the photos that inevitably follow. Second, they're flexible — the same format works for a baby's first birthday, an 18th, a 50th, or a 25th wedding anniversary. From our order book, that versatility is exactly why number cakes have moved from "trend" to "default" for milestone celebrations.

The format itself grew out of the "cream tart" trend that swept dessert tables a few years ago — layered biscuit or sponge cut to a shape and piled with cream and toppings. Number cakes took that idea and made it about the milestone. The same technique also makes letter cakes (an initial, or a short word like "LOVE" for an engagement), so once you understand the digit version you can apply it to almost any celebration. We make both, and the build and pricing logic is the same: each character is its own little slab of layered cake.

Which Ages and Numbers Do Sydney Families Order Most?

Milestone ages dominate number-cake orders, with the round numbers leading the way. From our internal order data across thousands of Sydney celebrations, the most-requested digits cluster around the 1st, 18th, 21st, 30th, 40th and 50th birthdays — the ages where the number itself is the headline of the party.

First birthdays are a category of their own. A single "1" surrounded by pastel cream and a few strawberries is one of our most photographed cakes, partly because it doubles as the centrepiece for the cake-smash photos parents plan around. At the other end, 50th and 60th cakes tend to be larger, more elegant, and often gold-accented — the same digit format, dressed up for an adult crowd.

How Big Should a Number Cake Be for Your Guest Count?

Match the digit size to your head-count: a single number serves roughly 10–20 people, while a double-digit pair serves about 20–40. Because each digit is essentially its own slab, two numbers side by side roughly double the servings — a "21" feeds far more than a single "2". From our experience, telling us your guest count first is the quickest way to land on the right dimensions.

The thing to remember is that a number cake's footprint is large but relatively thin compared with a tall round, so the serve count comes from area, not height. If you're feeding a big crowd, we can scale the digits up, add an extra layer of sponge for depth, or pair the number cake with a few dozen eggless cupcakes to extend it without losing the centrepiece effect.

One sizing nuance people miss: a number cake is usually richer per slice than a plain sponge round, because each portion carries cream filling between the layers plus a generous load of toppings. That means guests are often satisfied with a smaller piece, so the serving ranges above are deliberately conservative. If your crowd tends to graze rather than eat a full slice — common at evening events with other food around — you can size towards the lower end and still have plenty. When the cake is the main dessert, size towards the upper end instead.

Quick sizing guide

  • Single digit (e.g. "5"): ~10–20 serves. Great for younger kids' birthdays and intimate gatherings.
  • Double digit (e.g. "30"): ~20–40 serves. The standard for adult milestones.
  • Triple digit (e.g. "100"): a showstopper for a centenary or a big anniversary — ask us about scaling.
  • Number plus cupcakes: the easiest way to feed a large party while keeping the digit as the hero.

What Designs and Toppings Work Best on a Number Cake?

The best number cakes keep a clear theme and let the toppings do the talking. From our experience, the designs that land best pick one palette — soft pastels for a first birthday, deep reds and gold for a 50th — and repeat it across the cream, fruit and decorations. A busy number cake reads as cluttered; a restrained one looks expensive.

A pattern worth borrowing: the toppings should suit the eater, not just the photo. Kids' number cakes do well with chocolate pieces, sprinkles and a favourite character's colours, while adult milestones look their best with fresh berries, macarons, a few edible flowers and a single metallic accent. The flavour underneath matters too — Chocolate and Red Velvet hold a sculpted digit shape beautifully, and our 2026 design trends lean towards exactly this restrained, topping-led look.

Popular finishes we make all the time:

  • Cream-and-berry: piped cream rosettes with strawberries, raspberries and blueberries — fresh and timeless.
  • Chocolate-loaded: chocolate shards, malt balls and ganache drips for a Chocolate or Ferrero Rocher base.
  • Floral elegant: muted cream with edible flowers and a gold leaf accent — ideal for 40ths, 50ths and anniversaries.
  • Kids' character: bright colours and themed toppers matched to the birthday child's favourite.

One detail that lifts a number cake from nice to memorable is matching the toppings to the guest of honour rather than to a generic template. For a gardener's 60th, a scatter of edible flowers in their favourite colour; for a footy fan's 30th, the club's two colours echoed in the cream and berries; for a baby's first birthday, the nursery palette repeated on the cake. We're happy to work from a single reference photo or a colour you have in mind — send it through with your order and we'll build the look around it, so the cake feels made for the person, not pulled off a shelf.

Are Number Cakes Available 100% Eggless?

Yes — every number cake we make is 100% eggless, and that matters more each year. As of August 2025, the National Allergy Centre of Excellence's "Costly Reactions" report found that 8.2 million Australians — 30% of the population — now live with allergic disease, up from 4.1 million (19.6%) in 2007. Egg is a common childhood allergen, so an eggless showpiece means the birthday child and their friends can all dig in.

It's also about inclusion at the table. Many lacto-vegetarian Hindu and Jain families avoid eggs entirely, and a number cake is often the one dessert everyone gathers around for the candles. At Num Num's, eggless isn't a special request — it's the whole kitchen. One honest caveat: eggless is not allergen-free. Our cakes contain dairy and may involve nuts, and standard cakes contain wheat — see Food Standards Australia New Zealand for the full allergen list, and tell us about any severe allergy when you order.

How Far Ahead Should You Order a Number Cake in Sydney?

Order 4–5 days ahead wherever you can — a number cake is a custom build, not an off-the-shelf round. The minimum is 48 hours, but the sculpting, layering and topping work rewards a little lead time, and the busiest weekends book out first. From our experience, the smoothest milestone orders land in our WhatsApp a week before the party.

Festival weekends and the spring–summer birthday peak are the times to plan earliest. If your celebration falls near Diwali, Eid or the December rush, treat 4–5 days as the floor rather than the target. The earlier you confirm the digit, flavour and toppings, the more design freedom you have — and the less chance of a last-minute compromise on the centrepiece of the party.
