What Is Nowruz, and Why Does It Matter in Sydney?

Nowruz means "new day" in Persian. It marks the exact moment of the spring equinox. The United Nations recognised it as International Nowruz Day in 2010. It originated more than 3,000 years ago, making it one of the oldest continuously celebrated festivals in the world. It predates Islam by many centuries. UNESCO inscribed Nowruz on its Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2016. That listing recognises its shared significance across Iran, Afghanistan, Azerbaijan, Central Asia, parts of the Caucasus and the Balkans.

In Sydney, Nowruz is celebrated by a significant and growing Persian-speaking and Afghan community across Western Sydney. Kurdish, Azerbaijani, Tajik and other Central Asian households join in too. For many families, the festival is less about the calendar date and more about renewal. A thoroughly cleaned home, new clothes, and thirteen days of visiting relatives and friends matter most. A custom eggless cake has become a natural part of that hospitality. It's something to share when guests arrive, whatever their background or dietary needs. Is Nowruz a Religious Festival?

No — Nowruz is a cultural and seasonal celebration with roots in pre-Islamic, Zoroastrian Persia (UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage listing, 2016). It is not a religious festival tied to any single faith. Today, people of many faiths and no faith across the Persian-speaking and Central Asian world celebrate it. That makes it fundamentally different from festivals tied to one specific religion.

It's worth being precise here, since the distinction matters to the families who celebrate. Nowruz is not the same festival as Eid. Eid follows the Islamic lunar calendar and marks the end of Ramadan or the Hajj pilgrimage. Our separate guide to eggless cakes for Eid in Sydney covers that celebration in detail. It is also distinct from Diwali, the Hindu festival of lights. See our Diwali and Indian festivals guide for more. Nowruz stands on its own. It is a spring renewal festival. Many Iranian, Afghan, Kurdish, Azerbaijani and Tajik Australians mark it, no matter their faith.

[Unique Insight]

Most guides on this site anchor to one religion — Diwali to Hinduism, Eid to Islam, Lohri to Sikh and Hindu households. Nowruz breaks that pattern entirely. It has no religious gatekeeping at all. An Armenian Christian neighbour, a secular Iranian family and a Zoroastrian household can all share the same Haft-Sin table.

What Happens During a Sydney Nowruz Celebration?

A Sydney Nowruz celebration centres on the Haft-Sin table. It is an arrangement of seven symbolic items. Each Persian name begins with the letter "S". Among them: sabzeh (sprouted wheatgrass for rebirth), samanu (sweet pudding for fertility) and senjed (dried fruit for love). See the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage listing for the full set. The celebration period runs 13 days. It opens with the New Year moment itself. It closes with Sizdah Bedar, an outdoor picnic day when families release the sabzeh sprouts back into nature.

Sydney's version adapts naturally to the Southern Hemisphere calendar and to apartment and suburban living. Bonfires become fire pits or LED lanterns where open flame isn't practical. Extended family visits are a core part of the 13-day period. They often become a rotating schedule of dinners across Western Sydney lounge rooms and backyards. The spirit stays the same: hospitality, renewal and shared food. A cake increasingly takes its place as the centrepiece dessert, alongside traditional sweets like baklava, sohan and noghl.

Why Do Eggless Cakes Suit a Modern Haft-Sin Table?

Egg allergy affects approximately 9% of Australian infants. Eggs remain one of the eight priority food allergens regulated under the Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) code (Allergy & Anaphylaxis Australia). Unlike some festivals covered on this site, Nowruz carries no inherent religious rule against eating eggs. In fact, dyed and painted eggs are themselves a traditional Haft-Sin table decoration in many households. The appeal of an eggless cake here is practical rather than doctrinal.

A growing number of Sydney Nowruz hosts choose an eggless cake for one simple reason: it removes a variable from a large, mixed-age gathering. Guests can span grandparents to toddlers, vegetarian relatives, and the roughly one in ten households managing a food allergy of some kind. A cake where nobody needs to check the recipe card is one less thing to worry about. It also pairs well with the fresh, colourful, spring aesthetic of the Haft-Sin table itself. A decorated cake in soft pastels sits naturally beside hyacinths and painted eggs.

Our cakes contain dairy — milk, butter and cream are used throughout. So we describe ourselves accurately as an egg-free kitchen, not a vegan one. For guests managing a severe or anaphylactic egg allergy, we recommend stating your needs clearly when ordering. Please also follow your own medical or ASCIA-aligned advice. Our kitchen reduces, but cannot promise to eliminate, every trace of cross-contact from a shared commercial space.

What Should You Know About Chaharshanbe Suri and Sizdah Bedar?

Chaharshanbe Suri is the fire-jumping festival held on the last Tuesday night before Nowruz. Families traditionally leap over small bonfires. As they jump, they chant a phrase that asks the fire to take their paleness and give them its warmth and colour in return. In Sydney, open bonfires are rarely practical, so many households mark the night with fire pits, sparklers or simply candlelight and music instead. The symbolism matters more than the exact flame.

[Personal Experience]

Customers picking up a Chaharshanbe Suri or Nowruz cake often mention they're heading straight to a park for a family bonfire gathering afterwards. That's why we always ask about transport time when a design uses fresh cream or delicate piping. A firmer buttercream finish simply travels better to an outdoor picnic than a soft whipped topping.

Sizdah Bedar closes the celebration on the 13th day with an entirely different mood. Families head outdoors for a picnic, releasing their sabzeh sprouts into a park, river or garden to symbolically return the year's renewal to nature. It is considered unlucky to stay indoors on this day. Sydney's parks around late March and early April often fill with Persian, Afghan and Central Asian families as a result. They share food outdoors, and it's another natural occasion for a celebration cake, alongside picnic staples.
