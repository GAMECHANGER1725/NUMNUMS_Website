Eggless Cakes for Dussehra in Sydney: Celebrate Vijayadashami with Every Guest at the Table

By Tarun Patel 19 June 2026 9 min read Festival · Eggless Cakes

Dussehra — also known as Vijayadashami — is one of the most widely observed Hindu festivals in Australia. Celebrated on the tenth day of the month of Ashvin in the Hindu calendar (typically falling in October), it marks the victory of Rama over Ravana, of light over darkness, of good over evil. For Sydney's Indian community, it is a day of gathering: family thalis, cultural performances, puja at home, and increasingly, a special celebration cake that everyone at the table can share.

That last detail matters more than it might seem. When a Dussehra gathering draws together 20 or 30 people — many of them lacto-vegetarian Hindus who do not eat eggs — a conventional cake stops being the obvious choice. For observant Hindu households, eggs are excluded as a form of non-vegetarianism. Ordering an eggless cake isn't a workaround or a compromise: it is the only option that works for the whole table. At Num Num's Bakery, with two Sydney locations in Harris Park and Riverstone, every single cake on the menu is 100% eggless by default — no substitution, no special request, no asterisk. It's simply how we bake.

Sydney has one of Australia's largest Hindu communities, concentrated in suburbs across Parramatta, Harris Park, Blacktown, Seven Hills, and the Kellyville–Riverstone north-west corridor. For those families, a Dussehra cake order from Num Num's is becoming as routine as booking a puja priest or ordering a catered thali. This guide covers everything you need to know: which flavours work best for a Dussehra gathering, how far in advance to order, where to pick up from, and how the cakes meet Hindu vegetarian dietary standards.

Why Does Dussehra Call for an Eggless Cake?

According to the 2021 ABS Census, Sydney's Hindu population numbers over 148,000 — the largest of any Australian city — with particularly high concentrations in suburbs like Parramatta, Harris Park, Seven Hills, Blacktown, and the Kellyville–Riverstone corridor. For observant Hindu households, lacto-vegetarian practice excludes eggs as a form of non-vegetarian food. When you're hosting a Dussehra gathering of 20 or 30 people, most of your guests won't eat a conventional egg-based cake — making an eggless option not a preference but a hosting necessity.

The challenge that most hosts face is this: eggless baking is genuinely hard to get right. Eggs provide structure, moisture retention, and the lift that makes a cake light and airy. Reformulating without them — and achieving the same texture, crumb, and rise as a conventional cake — requires years of dedicated recipe development. Most general bakeries treat eggless as a substitution tier, producing results that look and taste slightly different from the "real" version. At Num Num's, there is no conventional version. Every recipe was designed eggless from the beginning, which is why our Dussehra cakes hold up alongside the traditional Indian sweets on the table — not as the "also-ran" option for dietary reasons, but as the centrepiece.

Sydney's Hindu community of over 148,000 residents (ABS Census 2021, abs.gov.au/census) is heavily concentrated in western and north-western suburbs. For lacto-vegetarian Hindu households hosting Dussehra gatherings, an eggless cake is not a dietary accommodation — it is the standard that the entire celebration is built around.

What distinguishes a Dussehra cake from a generic celebration cake is less about decoration and more about whether it belongs at the table. When the rest of the spread includes jalebi, ladoo, and kheer, you want a cake that complements those flavours rather than clashing with them. Rasmalai — with its fragrant cardamom and rose undertone — does exactly that. Butterscotch brings a warm caramel note that bridges Western cake tradition with South Asian sweetness. Are Num Num's Dussehra Cakes Suitable for Hindu Vegetarian Guests?

According to Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ), eggs are one of the eight priority food allergens in Australia — with strict labelling and cross-contamination obligations applying. But for the majority of Dussehra guests asking about eggless cakes, the concern is not allergy but religious and cultural dietary practice. Lacto-vegetarian Hindu practice excludes eggs on the grounds that they are a form of non-vegetarianism. For guests who keep this practice, eating an egg-containing cake served at a religious festival would be a meaningful transgression, not a preference.

At Num Num's Bakery, eggs are not present anywhere in the kitchen — not in any product, any preparation area, or any equipment. This is not simply a recipe reformulation. The entire bakery operates without eggs. For Dussehra hosts, this means you can serve a Num Num's cake to your most observant guests without hesitation on the egg axis.

It's important to be precise about what this does and does not cover:

  • Hindu vegetarian (lacto-vegetarian) households — suitable. Our cakes contain no eggs and are made in an egg-free kitchen.
  • Sikh dietary practice — suitable on the egg axis. Many Sikh dietary frameworks exclude eggs; our cakes meet this requirement.
  • Jain households — suitable on the egg axis. Jain dietary practice excludes eggs; our entire menu qualifies.
  • Egg allergy sufferers — suitable. The egg-free kitchen removes cross-contamination risk on the egg axis. For severe allergy, we recommend customers disclose at the time of ordering and follow guidance from ASCIA (allergy.org.au).
  • Vegan or plant-based guests — please note: our cakes contain dairy (milk powder, butter, cream). We are not a vegan bakery. For strictly plant-based guests, our cakes are not suitable and we advise hosts accordingly.

If you have specific allergen concerns beyond eggs — including dairy, gluten, or tree nuts — contact us directly via WhatsApp before placing your order. We'll provide a complete ingredient breakdown for your chosen flavour so you can make an informed decision for your guests.

Dussehra Cake Sizes — What Works for a Group Celebration?

Most Dussehra gatherings in Sydney fall into three size categories: intimate family (8–15 guests), medium gathering (20–35), and larger community event (50+). From our order experience across Sydney's Indian community, an 8-inch round serves 10–15 guests comfortably, a 10-inch serves 18–25 guests, and a 12-inch or two-tier arrangement works for gatherings of 30 or more.

One consideration particular to Dussehra gatherings: cake is rarely the only sweet on the table. When you're also serving ladoo, jalebi, barfi, or kheer, guests tend to have smaller portions of cake. This means you can often order a size down from what you'd choose for a standalone birthday party. If you're serving a cake alongside a full traditional sweets spread at a Dussehra gathering of 25, a 10-inch round will likely serve everyone well. When in doubt, WhatsApp us and we'll help you calibrate.

For larger Dussehra community events — cultural society gatherings, temple celebrations, or office events — a two-tier or multi-cake arrangement works well. Multiple smaller cakes of different flavours also give guests a choice, which can be particularly useful when you have a wide range of ages and preferences.
