Eggless Cake for a Nut Allergy: What "100% Egg-Free" Does — and Doesn't — Cover

By Tarun Patel 16 July 2026 11 min read Eggless Cakes · Dietary

You've found the eggless bakery. Now someone in the family also has a nut allergy, and the question gets harder to answer honestly: does a 100% egg-free cake automatically mean a nut-safe one too? It doesn't — and we'd rather tell you that plainly than let you assume otherwise. Num Num's Bakery is a dedicated 100% eggless kitchen, but it is not a certified nut-free facility, and a few of our flavours use nut ingredients directly.

Food allergy affects around 10% of Australian infants, 4–8% of children and roughly 2% of adults, and egg and peanut are consistently the two most common early-childhood triggers, according to the Australasian Society of Clinical Immunology and Allergy (ASCIA). When a family manages both allergies at once — which happens often — ordering a celebration cake takes real conversation, not a blanket "allergy-friendly" label.

How Common Are Egg and Nut Allergies in Australian Children?

Egg allergy affects about 9% of Australian infants and peanut allergy about 3%, according to the HealthNuts study run by the Murdoch Children's Research Institute, and having one allergy raises the chance of managing the other at the same time. That overlap is exactly why "eggless" and "nut-free" need to be treated as two separate questions, never one.

Egg and/or peanut allergies now affect roughly one in ten young Australian children, and more than 90% of childhood food allergy in this country traces back to just eight allergens — egg, milk, peanut, tree nuts, wheat, soy, fish and shellfish — according to the Centre for Food Allergy Research. For a family juggling both an egg allergy and a nut allergy, a bakery that only solved half the problem isn't much help at party time.

Egg allergy affects around 9% of Australian infants and peanut allergy around 3%, and the two commonly occur in the same child (HealthNuts Study, Murdoch Children's Research Institute). Because they are separate proteins with separate risks, a kitchen that has solved one does not automatically solve the other.

Does an Egg-Free Kitchen Mean Nut-Free Too?

No — an egg-free kitchen is not automatically a nut-free one, and no honest bakery should imply otherwise. Every cake at Num Num's is made without eggs anywhere on-site, in any product or piece of equipment, which genuinely removes the egg cross-contamination pathway. Nuts are a different ingredient class with a different risk profile, and we haven't made — and won't make — the same claim about them.

Our cakes contain dairy (milk powder, butter), so they are eggless but not vegan. Standard cakes contain wheat flour, so they are not gluten-free either — for that, see our separate guide to eggless and gluten-free cake options. Several flavours use or are prepared near nut ingredients, and our kitchen is a shared working space, not a segregated nut-free facility with its own dedicated equipment and air handling.

This is exactly why we ask every allergy-conscious customer to tell us clearly, every time, what they're managing — not just "eggless" but the full picture. It's a small extra step that makes a real difference to how carefully we prepare and pack your order.

What Does Australian Law Require Bakeries to Tell You About Nuts and Eggs?

Since 25 February 2024, Australian food businesses must declare priority allergens — including egg and each individual tree nut — in bold text under Standard 1.2.3 and Schedule 9 of the Food Standards Code, according to Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ). Tree nuts must be listed individually rather than lumped together — almond, Brazil nut, cashew, hazelnut, macadamia, pecan, pine nut, pistachio and walnut are all named separately, and egg from any bird species must be declared too.

That rule was built for packaged food labels, and a small-batch, made-to-order bakery like ours doesn't sell off a shelf — every cake is discussed with you directly before it's made. We still take the spirit of that law seriously: ask us about any flavour's ingredients and cross-contact risk, and we'll give you a straight answer rather than a marketing line. For general food-safety context in NSW, NSW Health is a useful additional resource.

In practice, this means we can tell you exactly which ingredients go into any flavour — whether hazelnut, dairy or wheat is involved — and we can tell you what else is being prepared in the kitchen on your pick-up day. What we can't do is issue the kind of formal allergen statement a packaged-food manufacturer signs off on, because a made-to-order cake isn't a packaged product. The honest version of allergen safety here is a conversation, repeated every time you order, not a one-off certificate.

Which Flavours Should Nut-Allergic Customers Approach with Caution?

Ferrero Rocher is the one flavour on our menu that nut-allergic customers should avoid or specifically ask about, because it's made using hazelnut — one of the eight tree nuts FSANZ requires food businesses to declare individually. We'd rather flag that up front than have you find out after the cake arrives.

The remaining 14 flavours — Chocolate, Vanilla, Red Velvet, Butterscotch, Black Forest, White Forest, Strawberry, Blueberry, Mango, Lychee, Pineapple, Tiramisu, Cookies & Cream and Rasmalai — don't use nuts as a headline ingredient. But because they're prepared in the same kitchen, on shared benches and equipment, we can't certify zero trace of nut on any of them. If you're managing a nut allergy, tell us which flavour you're considering and we'll talk through exactly how it's made that day, including whether Ferrero Rocher or nut-based garnishes are on the schedule at the same time.

How Do You Safely Order an Eggless, Nut-Conscious Cake?

Clear, repeated communication is the single biggest factor in safe allergen management, according to ASCIA, which recommends telling any food provider about an allergy every time you order — not assuming they remember from last time or that the recipe hasn't changed.

From our WhatsApp order data across 2025–26, a growing share of allergy-related enquiries now mention both an egg allergy and a nut allergy or nut-conscious request in the same message — families managing more than one restriction at once, ordering for a child's party or a shared family event.

  1. State both allergies upfront: Message +61 425 697 725 and name both the egg allergy and the nut allergy clearly, along with severity if you're comfortable sharing it.
  2. Ask about the specific flavour: Confirm ingredients and same-day cross-contact risk for your chosen flavour — not the menu in general.
  3. Confirm everything in writing: We'll reply on WhatsApp with what we can and can't guarantee, so there's a written record before you pay.

What Should Families Managing a Severe Nut or Egg Allergy Do Before Ordering?

For anaphylaxis-level allergy, ASCIA's guidance is clear: carry prescribed adrenaline injectors at all times, tell every food provider explicitly, and never assume a dish is safe without asking directly, according to the ASCIA Peanut, Tree Nut and Seed Allergy guidance. That advice applies just as much to a bakery cake as to a restaurant meal.

We can accommodate many requests and take real steps to reduce risk — choosing a flavour without nuts as an ingredient, being upfront about which cakes are scheduled near Ferrero Rocher that day, and talking through preparation with you before you order. What we cannot do is certify a nut-free kitchen or promise a zero-trace guarantee, because we are a shared working space, not a segregated allergen-controlled facility.

If you or your child has a diagnosed severe egg or nut allergy, please make the final call based on your own doctor's advice and your ASCIA action plan, not on anything written here. For general background on managing anaphylaxis risk day to day, Allergy & Anaphylaxis Australia is a genuinely useful, Australia-specific resource, alongside your allergist or GP.

Concretely, "accommodate" might mean baking your cake earlier in the day before any Ferrero Rocher order is prepared, using a fresh set of tools for your cake, or simply confirming that none of the ingredients in your chosen flavour touch nuts at any stage. It won't mean a guarantee, and we won't pretend otherwise just to win the order — a cautious "we can't be 100% certain, here's what we can do" is more useful to an allergic family than false reassurance.

Why Do Egg and Nut Allergies Often Overlap in the Same Child?

Roughly nine in ten children outgrow egg allergy by age six, compared with only around three in ten who outgrow peanut allergy by the same age, according to the HealthNuts study — one reason families so often manage both allergies at once during the exact years when birthday cakes matter most.

Egg allergy and nut allergy also share risk factors rather than one causing the other — early eczema, family history of allergic disease, and how allergens are first introduced in infancy all play a role, per ongoing research from the Murdoch Children's Research Institute. Having a nut allergy doesn't mean tree nuts and peanuts carry the same risk for you either: the Allergy & Anaphylaxis Australia peanut page notes that a peanut allergy doesn't automatically mean a tree nut allergy, since the proteins involved are different — another reason a general conversation with your own doctor beats any blanket assumption, ours included.

What this means practically: don't expect one allergy diagnosis to explain the other, and don't expect one "safe" bakery label to cover every allergen a family manages.
