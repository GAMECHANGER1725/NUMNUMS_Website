Eggless Cakes for Easter in Sydney: An Egg-Free Bakery for an Egg-Themed Holiday

By Tarun Patel 14 July 2026 10 min read Easter · Eggless Cakes

Easter might be the strangest possible holiday for an eggless bakery to be busy on. Chocolate eggs, egg hunts, hollowed-out shells hidden in the garden — the entire festival is built around eggs, and yet every April, Num Num's Bakery becomes one of the busiest cake counters in Sydney's north-west and west. The reason isn't a marketing gimmick. It's that a huge share of the families gathering for Easter lunch in Sydney don't eat eggs at all, and they still want a proper celebration cake on the table.

Australians are set to spend roughly $2.2 billion on Easter food and chocolate in 2025, with NSW households accounting for around $710 million of that and spending an average of $148 per person, according to Roy Morgan's 2025 Easter spending research. A slice of that spend goes on dessert — and for Hindu vegetarian households, Sikh families, egg-allergic guests and plenty of secular multicultural households besides, an eggless cake is the only cake that actually works for everyone at the table.

Why Do So Many Sydney Families Order Eggless Cakes for Easter?

Because Easter is a long-weekend gathering as much as a religious one, and an eggless cake is the one dessert that works no matter who's coming. NSW households plan to spend an average of $148 per person on Easter food in 2025, part of a national $2.2 billion Easter food and chocolate spend covering 15.2 million people, according to Roy Morgan. A cake bought for that occasion needs to suit a genuinely mixed table — grandparents, kids doing an egg hunt, a vegetarian aunt, a friend with a coeliac or egg-allergic child.

From our own order books, the pattern repeats every year: Easter weekend and the fortnight either side of it is one of our four busiest stretches, alongside Diwali, Christmas and birthday season. Families aren't ordering an eggless cake despite the egg-heavy theme of the holiday — they're ordering one because of it. When chocolate eggs and hot cross buns are everywhere, a cake that quietly removes the one ingredient a chunk of the guest list can't or won't eat is genuinely useful, not incidental.

Why Is an Eggless Bakery the Right Call for a Holiday Built Around Eggs?

Because the demand for egg-free food has never been higher, even on a holiday themed around eggs. As of August 2025, the National Allergy Centre of Excellence's "Costly Reactions" report found that 8.2 million Australians — 30% of the population — now live with allergic disease, double the 4.1 million (19.6%) recorded in 2007. Egg allergy is one of the most common childhood food allergies, and a lot of those children will be at an Easter egg hunt this year with a cake stand right next to it.

That's the irony that makes Num Num's busy every April: the same holiday that hides chocolate eggs in the backyard is also the holiday where an egg-free bakery gets the most calls. Parents managing an egg allergy don't want their child left out of the cake-cutting photo any more than they want them left out of the egg hunt. A 100% eggless kitchen means there's no cross-contamination risk to manage and no separate "safe" dessert quietly kept apart from everyone else's slice.

What we notice every Easter, more than at almost any other time of year, is how many customers open the WhatsApp conversation with "my child can't have eggs" rather than a flavour request. It's rarely the first thing people mention at a birthday order. At Easter, it usually comes first — probably because Easter itself puts eggs so visibly on the table that families are already thinking about it before they've even messaged us.

When Is Easter 2026 in Sydney, and How Do NSW School Holidays Affect Ordering?

Easter Sunday 2026 falls on 5 April, with Good Friday on 3 April and Easter Monday on 6 April, both public holidays across NSW. That long weekend lands inside Term 1: NSW Term 1 2026 runs through to Friday 11 April, and the two-week autumn school holidays then begin Monday 14 April, according to the NSW Department of Education's 2026 term dates. In other words, the Easter long weekend itself falls on a normal school week, and the bigger, two-week holiday break follows just over a week later.

That timing matters for ordering. Many Sydney families hold their main Easter lunch over the 3–6 April long weekend while school is still technically in session, then host a second round of extended-family visits or kids' holiday-program parties once the school break properly starts on 14 April. From our order books, that creates two separate peaks rather than one — the long weekend itself, and the following week or two once school holidays begin.

Is Easter Only a Christian Celebration in Multicultural Sydney?

No — a large share of Num Num's Easter orders come from households that aren't celebrating Easter as a religious holiday at all. Hinduism grew 55.3% nationally between 2016 and 2021 to 684,002 people, and Sikhism grew 67% over the same period to around 210,400 people, with about 39% of Australia's Hindu population living in Greater Sydney, according to the 2021 Census data published by the Australian Bureau of Statistics. Harris Park and Riverstone both sit inside that Greater Sydney catchment.

For a lot of these families, Easter isn't a religious event — it's a long weekend, a school-holiday marker, and an excuse for an egg hunt with the kids and a cake afterwards. Public holidays are public holidays regardless of faith, and a four-day weekend with the extended family together is reason enough for a celebration cake. That's a meaningfully different customer than the one ordering a Christmas cake, and it's part of why "eggless" resonates so strongly at Easter specifically: it's already the household's everyday eating pattern, not a special accommodation made once a year.

The same goes for secular and interfaith households who simply enjoy the tradition of chocolate eggs and a family lunch without any religious framing at all. Whatever the reason someone is gathering on the long weekend, the cake on the table is the same 100% eggless range — nobody has to ask, and nobody has to explain their dietary needs to be included.

What Size Easter Cake Do You Need for Your Gathering?

Size your cake to the guest list, not your eye — Easter long-weekend gatherings tend to run bigger than a normal Sunday lunch, because extended family often visits from out of area over the four-day break. From our order data, a 6-inch round serves roughly 8–10 people, an 8-inch covers 15–20, and a 10-inch round stretches to 30–40. For a big extended-family lunch over the long weekend, a two-tier cake or two separate flavours is often the smarter call than one oversized single tier.

A useful trick for Easter specifically: if you're hosting an egg hunt beforehand, kids tend to have already eaten some chocolate by cake time, so a slightly smaller kids' portion and a larger adult portion works well. If your gathering falls in the second wave once school holidays begin on 14 April, expect a longer guest list as families use the extra days to catch up — size up a step for that later gathering compared with the long weekend itself.

Are Eggless Easter Cakes Safe for Egg Allergies and Other Dietary Needs?

Yes, with an honest caveat. According to Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ), eggs are one of Australia's priority food allergens, and our entirely egg-free kitchen removes the egg cross-contamination pathway from every product we make. Egg allergy affects roughly 9% of Australian infants, according to Allergy & Anaphylaxis Australia — a meaningful share of the young guests at any Easter egg hunt.

Egg-free does not mean allergen-free, though, and we say this on every dietary-facing post for good reason. Our cakes contain dairy (milk powder, butter), may involve nuts, and standard cakes contain wheat. For a severe or anaphylactic allergy, tell us when you order and follow your own medical or ASCIA-aligned advice — we'll give you a full ingredient breakdown for your chosen flavour before you commit. For dietary comparisons beyond eggs, our guide on dairy-free vs eggless cakes and our dedicated post on ordering an eggless cake for egg allergy both go into more detail.

Beyond allergy management, an eggless kitchen also suits several dietary and cultural frameworks common across our Harris Park and Riverstone catchments: Hindu vegetarian households, Sikh families, Jain households, and halal-conscious customers who prefer to sidestep any cross-contamination questions altogether. Whatever brings a household to the Easter table, the same 15-flavour range is available to everyone.
