What Is Guru Nanak Jayanti (Gurpurab) and Why Does It Matter in Sydney?

Guru Nanak Jayanti marks the birth of Guru Nanak Dev Ji in 1469, the founder of Sikhism and the first of the ten Sikh Gurus, and it is the single most widely observed date across the Sikh calendar (Britannica). Celebrations begin roughly two days before the date itself, with an unbroken 48-hour reading of the Guru Granth Sahib known as the Akhand Path. The day before, a Nagar Kirtan procession carries the scripture through the streets with hymn-singing and music.

The observance closes with langar — the free community kitchen that serves a vegetarian meal to anyone who attends, regardless of faith or background. In Sydney, gurdwaras across the north-west hold their own version of this pattern each November, and the scale keeps growing. In 2025, the Glenwood Gurdwara — inside the area policed by Riverstone Police Area Command — held a Nagar Kirtan for a separate Gurpurab that drew hundreds of participants, a clear sign of how established these processions have become locally.

How Large Is Sydney's Sikh Community, and Where Is It Growing Fastest?

Sikhism grew 67% between 2016 and 2021 to reach 210,400 people nationally, making it Australia's fastest-growing major religion over that period — well ahead of Hinduism's 55.3% growth and more than seven times the country's overall 8.6% population growth, according to the Australian Bureau of Statistics, "Religious affiliation in Australia," 2021 Census. That surge is not spread evenly. It concentrates heavily in a handful of Sydney postcodes.

Blacktown City is one of the clearest examples. According to the Blacktown City community profile (id, 2021 Census data), 4.6% of local residents identify as Sikh — more than six times the Greater Sydney average of 0.7%. The suburbs ringing our Riverstone shop, including Blacktown, Riverstone, Quakers Hill, Schofields and Glenwood, sit inside one of the country's largest Punjabi communities, and Gurpurab is the busiest date on that community's calendar.

Why Do Sikh Families Choose 100% Eggless Cakes for Gurpurab?

Many Sikh and Punjabi households follow a lacto-vegetarian diet that excludes eggs entirely, so a cake made without eggs can be shared at a Gurpurab gathering without anyone checking the ingredients first (Food Standards Australia New Zealand lists egg as one of only eight allergens that must always be declared on Australian food labels). That single fact shapes how a lot of families in Blacktown, Riverstone and Quakers Hill choose where to order from each November.

A cake is never a substitute for karah parshad, the ghee-and-flour sweet blessed and shared at every gurdwara service, or for the langar meal that anchors the whole day. It sits alongside them, at home, after the gurdwara visit — a modern addition to a tradition that already runs on generosity and shared food. Getting the eggless part right matters because a poorly reformulated recipe dries out fast and falls flat, which is exactly the risk when a bakery treats "eggless" as a one-off swap rather than the whole kitchen's standard.

Egg is one of Australia's eight priority food allergens under national food standards law, and reformulating a cake recipe without it — rather than simply omitting the egg from a standard recipe — takes sustained testing to get the crumb, rise and moisture right (FSANZ, food allergen labelling guidance). Num Num's rebuilt every recipe from scratch rather than substituting a single ingredient.
