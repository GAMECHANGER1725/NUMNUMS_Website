What Counts as a Custom Cake?

A custom cake is one built to your brief rather than picked off a fixed menu. You choose the flavour, size, theme, colour palette, finish and any message or figures — and the bakery designs and bakes to match. That can be as simple as a particular colour of buttercream with a name piped on top, or as involved as a sculpted, multi-tier centrepiece for a wedding.

The difference from a standard cake is the brief. A standard cake is a known product you order as-is; a custom cake is a one-off made for your specific occasion. At Num Num's, both come from the same egg-free kitchen and the same 15 flavours — the custom part is the design layered on top. You can see the core range on the Our Cakes page as a starting point for your own brief.

A custom cake is defined by its brief, not its difficulty — any cake made to your chosen flavour, size, theme and finish qualifies. The design can range from a single piped name to a sculpted tiered centrepiece, but the order always starts the same way: with a clear description of the occasion and the look you want.

Knowing which camp your idea falls into helps set the lead time and budget.

Which Flavour Should You Pick for Your Custom Cake?

From our internal order data, Chocolate is the safest crowd-pleaser for a custom cake — but the right flavour really depends on the occasion and who's eating. A kids' party leans differently to a 50th birthday or a wedding, and matching the flavour to the room is part of getting a custom cake right.

Here's how our 15 eggless flavours tend to map to occasions:

  • Kids' parties — Chocolate, Butterscotch, Strawberry and Cookies & Cream are the reliable winners; fun, familiar and rarely refused.
  • Adult milestone birthdays — Ferrero Rocher, Tiramisu and Red Velvet feel premium without being fussy, ideal for an 18th, 21st, 30th or 50th.
  • Weddings & engagements — Vanilla, Red Velvet and Black or White Forest are elegant, photograph well and suit a mixed guest list.
  • Cultural & festival celebrations — Rasmalai and Mango bridge a Western-style cake and South Asian sweets beautifully for Diwali, Eid and naming days.
  • Mixed or unknown crowds — when in doubt, Chocolate or Vanilla never misses, and both take colour and decoration cleanly.

You can also split flavours in a tiered cake — a different sponge per tier — so one custom cake covers more than one preference. See the full range on the Our Cakes page before you finalise your brief.

How Far Ahead Should You Order a Custom Cake?

Lead time depends on complexity. A minimum of 48 hours covers simpler designs, but detailed custom cakes need 4–5 days, and tiered or wedding cakes are best booked 1–2 weeks ahead. The single biggest cause of disappointment is leaving it too late for an elaborate design during a busy week.

Timing also matters around the calendar. School holidays and festivals are our peak periods, and slots fill fast. In 2026, NSW autumn school holidays run 7–17 April, winter 6–17 July, and spring 28 September–9 October, according to the NSW Department of Education — prime time for kids' parties. Festival weeks like Diwali, Eid and Christmas book out even earlier, so order ahead.

One pattern worth knowing: the last week of each school term and the first weekend of the holidays are consistently our busiest birthday windows. If your child's party lands there, treat the lead times above as a minimum and add a couple of days. A short message to reserve a slot — even before you've finalised the design — is the safest way to guarantee your date.

What Size Custom Cake Do You Need?

Match the cake to your guest count, then size up if you want leftovers. As a working guide from our orders, a 6-inch round serves roughly 8–12, an 8-inch serves 16–24, a 10-inch serves 30–40, and a two-tier cake handles 50–70 guests. For a children's party, remember the adults eat cake too — count the whole room, not just the kids.

How Much Does a Custom Cake Cost in Sydney?

Price tracks three things: size, design complexity and flavour — not a single flat rate. A larger cake uses more ingredients and oven time, but the bigger driver is usually decoration. A simple buttercream finish sits at one end; hand-piped detail, fondant figures, edible photo prints and tiered structures all add labour and cost.

From years of quoting cakes, the most budget-effective approach is to keep the cake size matched to your guest count and concentrate your spend on one striking feature rather than covering the whole cake in detail. A clean finish with a single bold element — a fresh-flower crown, a gold drip, a sculpted topper — usually photographs better and costs less than an all-over busy design. Because eggless is simply how we bake, you're never paying an extra surcharge for an egg-free request.

For an exact quote, send your design brief, guest count and a reference photo on WhatsApp, and we'll price it before you commit. There's no obligation — plenty of customers message us to sense-check a budget before they decide. If your idea is more elaborate than your budget allows, we'll suggest a simpler version that keeps the same impact, so you always leave with a clear, honest price rather than a guess. It's the same approach whether you're ordering a small birthday cake or a tiered centrepiece.

Why Choose an Eggless Custom Cake?

An eggless custom cake serves everyone at the party without a separate cake on the side. That matters more than it used to: as of August 2025, the National Allergy Centre of Excellence reported 8.2 million Australians — 30% — live with allergic disease, double the 2007 figure. Egg is a common childhood allergen, so at a kids' party, an egg-free cake quietly removes a real risk.

There's a cultural dimension too. Many Hindu, Jain and Sikh families are lacto-vegetarian and avoid eggs entirely, and according to Food Standards Australia New Zealand, egg is a declared priority allergen requiring strict labelling. Because Num Num's never uses eggs anywhere in the kitchen, an eggless custom cake is genuinely inclusive — and it doesn't cost you a thing in design, taste or texture. Our cakes do contain dairy, so they're egg-free but not vegan.
