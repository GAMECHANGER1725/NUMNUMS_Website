Cake Serving Size Guide: How Many People Does Each Cake Feed?

By Tarun Patel 20 July 2026 10 min read Custom Cakes · Sizing Guide

Ordering a cake is the easy part. The question that stalls almost every plan is the one nobody wants to guess wrong: how many people will this cake actually feed? Order too small and the last few guests miss out on dessert entirely. Order too large and you're wrapping slices in foil for a week. This guide gives you the real numbers — party-style and wedding-style, round and square, single-tier and stacked — so you can size a cake correctly the first time, for a birthday, a wedding or a corporate event anywhere in Sydney.

Here's the short version up front: an 8-inch round cake feeds about 16 to 20 people cut into generous party slices, and a 10-inch feeds roughly 24 to 30. Everything else — shape, tiers, wedding-style thin cuts, kids versus adults — adjusts around those two anchor sizes. The full breakdown is below, including a table you can screenshot before you order.

How Many People Does a 6-Inch, 8-Inch, 10-Inch or 12-Inch Cake Feed?

A 6-inch round cake feeds roughly 8 to 12 people, an 8-inch feeds 16 to 20, a 10-inch feeds 24 to 30, and a 12-inch feeds 40 to 50, all cut into standard party slices. These figures track the servings conventions used across the wedding and bakery industry, and they're the numbers our own team quotes from every day at Harris Park and Riverstone.

Two inches of diameter is not "a little bigger." A 10-inch cake holds nearly 60% more surface area than an 8-inch, which is why the jump in servings between sizes feels bigger than the size difference suggests. If you're hovering between two sizes for a guest list that might grow, size up — the cost difference is modest, and a slightly larger cake rescues a party far better than a slightly smaller one strands it.

What's the Difference Between Party-Style and Wedding-Style Slices?

A wedding-style slice is cut roughly 1 inch wide against a party slice's generous 1.5 to 2 inches, which is why the same cake can be quoted two different serving counts depending on the event. A 10-inch round that feeds 24 to 30 as party slices comfortably stretches to 34 to 38 as thin wedding cuts — the cake doesn't change, only the width of the knife marks does.

So which style should you plan for? Party-style suits a birthday, a family gathering or a work morning tea, where guests expect a proper, satisfying slice and second helpings are welcome. Wedding-style suits a formal reception plated alongside other desserts, or any event where the cake is one course among several rather than the main event. Does a Square Cake Really Serve More People Than a Round Cake?

Yes — an 8-inch square cake typically serves around 30 to 45% more guests than an 8-inch round of the same width, because a round loses cake to its curved edge and a small, awkward centre core, while a square cuts edge to edge with almost no waste. It's simple geometry: a round tray wastes the corners a square doesn't have.

The trade-off is presentation, not portions. A round cake reads as the celebratory centrepiece most people picture for a birthday or anniversary, while a square or rectangular slab is the practical choice when serving speed and sheer numbers matter more than a showpiece — think an office farewell, a school fundraiser, or a large corporate event. For milestone moments, most of our customers still choose round or tiered and accept the slightly lower yield; for big, casual crowds, a square or slab wins on value every time.

[Unique Insight]

A pattern we don't see discussed much: customers planning a corporate morning tea for 30-plus almost always underestimate how much a slab or square cake stretches their budget. Swapping a round 10-inch for an 8-inch square at the same guest count often frees up room in the brief for a nicer finish or an extra flavour, because the shape itself does the heavy lifting on servings.

How Does Serving Work for a Two-Tier or Three-Tier Cake?

A two-tier cake built from an 8-inch and a 10-inch layer serves around 45 to 55 guests as party slices — more than either tier feeds on its own — while a three-tier 6-8-10 stack pushes that to roughly 60 to 70. Tiers add height and presence to the table, and crucially, they let you offer two or three flavours from one cake, which is why they're the default choice for a Sydney wedding or a 40-plus milestone birthday.

[Unique Insight]

The part people miss when they order a tiered cake: it isn't just a bigger cake, it's two or three different cakes stacked together. A Chocolate base with a Rasmalai or Vanilla top means a mixed guest list gets a genuine choice, and nobody sighs "I wish we'd got chocolate" halfway through the afternoon. It's the single easiest upgrade for a mixed-flavour crowd.

Tiered cakes need more lead time and a sturdier transport plan than a single round, so build in a few extra days and a flat boot space for pick-up. Browse the full range of designs on our tiered cakes guide, or keep things simpler with a single 12-inch round, which still comfortably feeds a large party if a confident hand is doing the cutting.

How Many Guests Should You Plan for at a Birthday, Wedding or Corporate Event?

The average Australian household held just 2.5 people at the 2021 Census, down from 2.6 in 2016 (ABS, Household and Families Census, 2021). That's exactly why almost every celebration pulls together several households at once — a birthday for 20 to 25 guests, a backyard wedding for 60, or an office morning tea for 30 are all bigger than any single family, so size your cake to the actual guest list, not to how many people live under one roof.

[Personal Experience]

From our order history across Harris Park and Riverstone, kids' birthday parties of 20 to 25 guests most often land on an 8-inch or 9-inch round, milestone adult birthdays of 30-plus regularly step up to a 10-inch or 12-inch, and corporate orders for 40 to 60 people overwhelmingly choose a slab or two-tier cake over one oversized round — it simply cuts faster in an office kitchen.

Isn't it tempting to just round down to save money? Resist it. Families who run short are almost always the ones who sized for the guest list they invited, not the one who actually turns up plus their partners and kids. For a kids' birthday, count the adults standing around the table too — they eat full slices. For a milestone celebration or a corporate function, add a 10 to 15% buffer for second helpings and unexpected guests.
