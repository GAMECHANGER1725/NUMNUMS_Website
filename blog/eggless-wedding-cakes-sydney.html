Eggless Wedding Cakes in Sydney: The Complete Planning Guide

6 April 2026 10 min read Weddings · Sydney

The wedding cake is one of the most visible elements of the entire reception. It's photographed, cut in front of every guest, served as the final shared moment before the evening ends, and often remembered longer than any other part of the catering. For couples planning a wedding in Sydney where a significant portion of the guest list follows vegetarian or Jain dietary practices, the eggless wedding cake is not a niche option — it's a necessity.

And yet, finding a specialist eggless wedding cake in Sydney that doesn't feel like a compromise is harder than it should be. Most wedding cake studios are not eggless by design — they offer egg-free as a substitution, often with a narrowed flavour menu and less experience with the specific techniques required for a well-structured multi-tier eggless cake.

This guide is for couples who want to plan their eggless wedding cake properly — from understanding what makes a good one, to choosing the right flavours, to knowing exactly what to communicate when ordering from Num Num's Bakery in Harris Park or Riverstone.

Why an Eggless Wedding Cake Matters More Than You Think

The Guest List Reality

At an Australian wedding with South Asian heritage, the guest list will typically include a mix of backgrounds: Indian Australian families (often with significant proportions of vegetarian households), Australian-born guests, and international visitors. Within the South Asian contingent, it's common for a large portion — sometimes the majority — to not eat eggs as part of their regular practice.

A wedding cake that contains eggs means that a significant share of your guests either cannot eat it, choose not to, or eat it uncomfortably knowing it doesn't meet their dietary practice. For a ceremony as symbolic as cutting the wedding cake in front of everyone, that matters. An eggless cake means every single person at the table can participate fully in the moment.

It's Not a Compromise — When It's Done Right

The misconception about eggless cakes is that they taste inferior — that "eggless" is a caveat, a flag for something that didn't quite make it. That's true of eggless cakes where the recipe wasn't developed with care. It's not true of cakes where the eggless recipe is the primary recipe — the thing the baker has been refining and improving since the beginning.

Num Num's Bakery has been 100% eggless since it was founded in 2019. Every recipe was written as an eggless recipe — not adapted from a conventional one. The result is a cake that isn't "almost as good as." It's simply a good cake, made well, with no eggs in it.

Choosing Your Wedding Cake Flavours

The Tier Flavour Strategy

Multi-tier wedding cakes give couples the opportunity to serve more than one flavour, which is a real advantage at a diverse wedding. The most thoughtful approach is to use tiers to cover different preferences within the guest list:

  • Top tier (smallest): A signature or sentimental flavour — often Rasmalai for South Asian couples, or whatever means something personally to the couple.
  • Middle tier: A crowd-pleaser that works across all backgrounds — Chocolate, Red Velvet, or Butterscotch.
  • Base tier (largest): The flavour that will serve the most people — should be reliably excellent and broadly appealing.

The Most Popular Wedding Combination

The most frequently ordered combination for South Asian weddings is: Rasmalai (top tier), Chocolate (middle tier), Butterscotch (base tier). This covers cultural authenticity, universal appeal, and the crowd-pleasing richness that rounds out a reception menu. It also photographs well — three distinct fillings visible in the cut creates a striking moment.

Sizing Your Wedding Cake

Portion Estimates by Guest Count

Cake portion sizes at weddings vary depending on whether cake is served as a standalone dessert, one of several desserts, or a small ceremonial slice alongside other sweets. The following are estimates based on standard dessert-sized portions:

  • Up to 50 guests: Two-tier cake
  • 50–100 guests: Three-tier cake
  • 100–150 guests: Three-tier with larger base or supplementary sheet cake
  • 150+ guests: Four-tier or custom arrangement — discuss with the team

If cake is accompanied by a full mithai spread or other desserts, guests typically eat smaller portions of each — which means you can sometimes go one tier down from the standard estimate. If the cake is the centrepiece dessert without other sweets, err upward.

Designing Your Wedding Cake

How Custom Decoration Works

Every Num Num's Bakery cake is made to order, which means the decoration is not selected from a fixed catalogue — it's determined by the brief you provide. For wedding cakes, the more detail you include in your WhatsApp message, the closer the result will match your vision.

Elements to include in your decoration brief:

  • Colour palette: Primary and accent colours. Include hex codes if you're matching to wedding branding, or reference your florals.
  • Finish: Smooth buttercream, textured ruffles, ombré gradient, naked cake style — specify what appeals to you.
  • Toppers: Fresh flowers, monogram initials, fondant figurines — let the team know what you're considering.
  • Text: Names and date, or keep it unadorned — both are valid choices.
  • Reference images: A Pinterest board or two saved images sent via WhatsApp will communicate more efficiently than a paragraph description.

What Works Well at South Asian Weddings

For Indian, Sri Lankan, and South Asian weddings, certain decoration approaches work particularly well. Gold leaf detailing against a white or ivory base is both elegant and culturally resonant. Deep red, burgundy, or marigold orange are wedding colours with strong cultural significance. Florals — particularly marigold-inspired decoration or rose patterns — connect visually to both the traditional flower garland and the modern aesthetic of contemporary South Asian weddings in Sydney.

If your wedding has a colour theme — and most South Asian weddings do — bring that palette to the cake. A cake that echoes the lehenga colour, the mandap florals, or the table centrepieces creates a visual coherence that elevates the entire reception aesthetic.

Collecting and Transporting Your Wedding Cake

Wedding cakes require careful transport. A few practical notes:

  • Collect in a flat-based vehicle, ideally with air conditioning running — not the boot of a car in Australian summer heat.
  • Have the car ready before you pick up — minimise the time the cake sits outside in the heat, particularly for outdoor summer weddings.
  • Refrigerate at the venue until approximately 30–45 minutes before serving. Buttercream softens in heat but firms back up when chilled.
  • The pick-up window at Harris Park (open till 10 pm) is useful for same-day weddings where set-up happens in the afternoon or evening.
