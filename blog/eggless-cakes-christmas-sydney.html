A Sydney Christmas doesn't look like a postcard from the northern hemisphere. It's 30 degrees, the backyard's full, and the dessert table has to please a guest list that might span four cultures and three generations. For a growing number of families, it also has to be egg-free — and that's where most bakeries fall down. Num Num's Bakery is 100% eggless across both shops, Harris Park and Riverstone, so the centrepiece cake works for everyone at the table without a second thought.

Why Choose an Eggless Christmas Cake This Year?

In 2025, the National Allergy Centre of Excellence found that 8.2 million Australians — 30% of the population — now live with allergic disease, double the 4.1 million (19.6%) recorded in 2007 (NACE, Costly Reactions report, August 2025). Egg is one of the most common childhood allergens, affecting roughly 9% of Australian infants (Allergy & Anaphylaxis Australia). At a big family Christmas, the odds that someone at the table avoids eggs are higher than most hosts assume.

Then there's the cultural layer. Many Hindu, Jain, and other vegetarian households exclude eggs entirely, so a conventional Christmas cake quietly leaves part of the room out. An eggless cake flips that: it's the one dessert everyone can eat, from the egg-allergic toddler to the vegetarian grandparent. Inclusion, not compromise — and nobody has to be the person who asks.

Which Flavours Work Best for a Sydney Christmas?

From our internal December order data, the festive favourites split into two camps: traditional showpieces and fresh summer flavours. Black Forest and White Forest lead the "looks like Christmas" group with their cherry-and-cream finish, while Mango and Strawberry win with families who want something light after a hot lunch. There's no single right answer — it depends on whether your Christmas leans classic or summery.

Here's a pattern worth knowing: the cake you'd pick for a cold-climate Christmas isn't always the one that survives a Sydney December. Heavy chocolate ganache can soften fast at a 35-degree backyard lunch, so many of our regulars switch to fruit-forward flavours — Mango, Pineapple, Strawberry — or keep a richer Ferrero Rocher or Tiramisu cake chilled until the moment it's served. Plan around the heat, not against it.

The cake stops being a thing you check and becomes a thing everyone enjoys.

The earlier your WhatsApp lands, the more collection slots you can choose from.

Timing also overlaps with the school calendar. Ordering before the holidays start means you're ahead of the rush, not in it.

From our internal order history, the seven days from 18–24 December are by far the heaviest of our year — collection slots on the 23rd and 24th fill first, often days ahead. The takeaway is simple: lock in the date early, finalise the design later.

What Size Eggless Christmas Cake Do You Need?

From our internal order data, the most common Christmas sizing mistake is going too small — festive lunches grow, and a cake bought for ten ends up feeding twenty. As a working rule, allow one slice per guest plus a few spare, and round up rather than down. A cake that's a little too big becomes Boxing Day leftovers; one that's too small leaves people waiting.

Here's the rough guide our staff use when a customer isn't sure. A 6-inch round serves around 8–10 dessert portions — fine for a small family lunch. An 8-inch round covers roughly 15–20, the most popular Christmas size. A 10-inch handles 30–40, and for a larger gathering a two-tier cake scales comfortably beyond that. If your Christmas Day spans extended family and a steady stream of drop-in visitors, size up to the next tier.

  • 8-inch round — 15–20 portions, the classic Christmas size

Can Eggless Cakes Cater to Allergies and Cultural Diets at Christmas?

Yes — with one important honesty caveat. Every Num Num's cake is made in a 100% egg-free kitchen, which removes the egg cross-contamination pathway that worries families managing an egg allergy, and aligns with Hindu vegetarian, Jain, and many other egg-avoiding diets. Egg is one of the priority allergens that must be declared on Australian food labels (Food Standards Australia New Zealand), and an egg-free kitchen takes it off the table entirely.

What an eggless cake is not is allergen-free in every sense. Our cakes contain dairy (milk powder, butter), may involve nuts, and standard cakes contain wheat. So an eggless cake is a genuine solution for egg allergy and egg-free diets — but it isn't automatically safe for someone with a dairy, nut, or gluten allergy.

For a severe or anaphylactic egg allergy, please tell us when you order and follow your own medical and ASCIA-aligned advice — we'll talk you through the ingredients of your chosen flavour so you can make an informed decision for your Christmas table. For any allergy beyond eggs, message us first and we'll give you a full ingredient breakdown.
