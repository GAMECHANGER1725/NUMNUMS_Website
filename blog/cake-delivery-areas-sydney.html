Cake Delivery Areas in Sydney: Every Suburb We Serve

By Tarun Patel 21 July 2026 13 min read Cake · Delivery Areas

Search "cake shop near me" from almost anywhere between Parramatta and Riverstone and you'll get dozens of results — but not all of them make eggless cakes, and even fewer make every cake eggless as standard. Num Num's Bakery runs two shops, Harris Park and Riverstone, and between them we've published a dedicated local guide for suburbs stretching from the Parramatta CBD to the Hawkesbury fringe. This page is the map: every catchment, every cluster of suburbs, and a link straight to your area's guide.

We split coverage into two catchments because that's genuinely how the two shops work — not as a marketing device, but because it reflects which kitchen is realistically closest for each part of Sydney. Below, you'll find the Harris Park catchment (Parramatta and Cumberland), the Riverstone catchment (Blacktown and the north-west), and a short explanation of how ordering and collection work across both.

Why Split Sydney Into Two Catchments Instead of One Citywide Zone?

The two catchments exist because the population and geography genuinely split that way, not because of an arbitrary line on a map. As of the 2023 Estimated Resident Population, City of Parramatta had 269,145 residents. Cumberland Council had a further 245,323. That's a combined 514,468 people within the Harris Park catchment's core council areas alone, before counting the Ryde and inner-west fringe suburbs we also guide from Harris Park.

The Riverstone catchment centres on Blacktown City, which reached 426,202 residents by June 2023 and is growing at 3.71% annually. In the 2023–24 financial year, ABS Regional Population data recorded Box Hill–Nelson growing by 18,386 people (27%) and Marsden Park–Shanes Park by around 20% — both suburbs sit inside our Riverstone catchment, and both are among the fastest-growing statistical areas in the entire country.

In the 2023–24 financial year, Box Hill–Nelson grew by 18,386 people (27%), one of the fastest growth rates recorded anywhere in Australia (ABS, Regional Population 2023–24). For a bakery, that kind of growth means more new residents each year who haven't yet found their nearest eggless cake shop — which is exactly why a suburb-by-suburb guide matters more here than in a slower-growing area.

Which Suburbs Fall Inside the Harris Park / Parramatta Catchment?

The Harris Park catchment runs from the Parramatta CBD out through Cumberland and up to the Ryde fringe. Every suburb below is a genuine drive of well under half an hour from our Harris Park kitchen. We group it into four clusters below, because "45 suburbs" as a flat list tells you nothing about the area. Grouped by the roads and communities that actually connect them, it does.

Parramatta Core.

This is the shop's home turf, all within a 10-minute radius: Parramatta, Harris Park, Rosehill, Camellia, Granville, Clyde and Rydalmere. Most of our walk-in and short-notice pickups come from this cluster.

Cumberland Belt.

This cluster sits south and west of Parramatta, in the Cumberland Council area. It connects mainly via Woodville Road and the Hume Highway, and covers Auburn, Lidcombe, Berala, Regents Park, Merrylands, Holroyd, Guildford, Guildford West, South Granville, Woodville, Yennora and Chester Hill. This is one of Sydney's most multicultural corridors. A significant share of our Diwali, Eid and wedding-season orders come from here.

Westmead–Toongabbie Corridor.

This is the biggest single cluster, running north and west along the Great Western Highway and Old Windsor Road. It's also the one that borders the Riverstone catchment most closely. It covers Westmead, Northmead, North Parramatta, Constitution Hill, Winston Hills, Toongabbie, Old Toongabbie, Wentworthville, Greystanes, Girraween, Pemulwuy, Pendle Hill, Mays Hill, Oatlands, Dundas Valley and Telopea.

Ryde–Inner West Fringe.

Further east, still comfortably closer to Harris Park than Riverstone: Eastwood, Epping, Carlingford, North Rocks, Homebush, Strathfield, Ermington, Melrose Park, Newington and Silverwater. See our broader western Sydney cake shop guide for more on this wider area.

Which Suburbs Fall Inside the Riverstone / North-West Catchment?

The Riverstone catchment covers Sydney's fastest-growing corridor, running from Blacktown City through the Hills District to the Hawkesbury fringe. From our Riverstone shop, every suburb below is within a realistic same-morning collection window. As above, we've grouped it into four clusters rather than one long list.

Riverstone–Schofields Growth Corridor.

The newest housing estates in Sydney, built around the Sydney Metro Northwest line: Riverstone, Schofields, The Ponds, Marsden Park, Vineyard, Grantham Farm, Colebee and Box Hill. Several of these suburbs didn't exist as distinct postcodes a decade ago.

Rouse Hill–Kellyville–Hills District.

This is a more established, family-dense part of the Hills Shire, reached east along Windsor Road and Old Northern Road. It covers Rouse Hill, Tallawong, Kellyville, Kellyville Ridge, Beaumont Hills, Bella Vista, Castle Hill, Baulkham Hills and Glenhaven.

Blacktown–Quakers Hill Belt.

This is the most populous cluster in the catchment, running south along Sunnyholt Road and Richmond Road. It covers Quakers Hill, Blacktown, Seven Hills, Rooty Hill, Prospect, Kings Park, Plumpton, Marayong, Dean Park and Acacia Gardens.

Outer North-West / Hawkesbury Fringe.

The furthest reaches of the catchment, still a manageable drive for a planned pickup: Stanhope Gardens, Parklea, Glenwood, Kings Langley, Dural, Annangrove, Richmond and Windsor. For a wider view of this whole region, see our north-west Sydney eggless cakes guide.

[Personal Experience]

From taking orders across both catchments, we've noticed a pattern. The Riverstone side skews more towards weekend collection for kids' birthday parties. Harris Park sees a steadier flow of weekday pickups tied to office celebrations and cultural events near Parramatta CBD. Neither pattern is fixed — it's just what our order data has shown since Riverstone opened.

How Does Collection and Delivery Actually Work Across These Areas?

Every one of the 80 suburbs above follows the same ordering process, regardless of catchment: message us on WhatsApp, confirm your flavour and pickup time, then collect from whichever shop is closest. Num Num's is primarily click-and-collect, which keeps a fragile, fresh-cream cake intact far better than a courier box does. We've written a full breakdown of courier delivery versus click-and-collect, and how to transport a cake safely in your own car, in our dedicated cake delivery in Sydney guide. This page focuses on where we cover, not how the handoff itself works.

  1. Find your catchment: use the suburb lists above to confirm whether Harris Park or Riverstone is genuinely closer to you.
  2. Browse flavours: check the Our Cakes page for the full 15-flavour range — every single one is 100% eggless.
  3. Message your order: send your date, design brief and preferred shop to +61 425 697 725 on WhatsApp.
  4. Collect fresh: pick up at your confirmed time from Harris Park or Riverstone — both have easy on-site or street parking.

You can also check your own route on Google Maps before you order, since traffic on the M4 and Windsor Road corridors varies a lot by time of day. Once you know which shop suits your suburb, the Order Online page has full sizing guidance for parties of any size.

How Far in Advance Should Each Catchment Order?

Lead time depends on the cake, not the catchment — a standard cake needs at least 48 hours' notice from either Harris Park or Riverstone, based on our internal order data across both shops in 2025–26. Custom, tiered or heavily decorated cakes, and anything ordered during Diwali, Eid, Christmas or NSW school holidays, need 4–5 days regardless of which suburb you're collecting from.

The one real difference between catchments is when demand peaks. Riverstone's newer growth suburbs — Marsden Park, The Ponds, Box Hill — tend to spike around weekend birthday parties. Harris Park's Cumberland and Parramatta suburbs see a steadier midweek flow tied to cultural events and office celebrations instead. Neither pattern changes the minimum notice required.
